1. Recalculate free cash flow yourself. Don't use the company's reported figure. Start with cash from operating activities, subtract CapEx, then subtract:

Tax withholding payments on RSU vesting (disclosed in the cash flow statement or equity section)
The portion of buybacks that are dilution-offset (look at the Statement of Stockholders' Equity for granularity — Meta's is apparently better than most)

2. Look at the equity statement, not just the income statement. The Statement of Stockholders' Equity shows share issuances, buybacks, and RSU-related activity in more detail than the headline numbers.
3. Ask: what would I pay if I owned 100% of this business? If you removed SBC and had to pay engineers in cash salary instead, how much would earnings drop? That's the economic reality.
4. Be sceptical of analyst out-year projections. 2029–2030 free cash flow estimates for hyperscalers are doing a lot of work in current valuations, and those estimates may be based on an optimistic assumption that CapEx tapers sharply and unit economics improve dramatically.
5. Watch debt levels carefully. If a company's true free cash flow is much lower than reported, but CapEx is still rising, they have to fund the gap somewhere. Rising debt in this context is a yellow flag, not normal corporate finance.
6. SBC isn't a non-cash expense in the way it sounds. The cash does leave the building — just through buybacks and tax withholding rather than payroll. Treat it as an operating cost.
